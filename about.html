How a pillar is chosen

The whole method, in the open

Trust does not come from a logo or a ceremony. It comes from a method anyone can read and no one can quietly bend. This is that method, in plain words. The full charter and rules of procedure sit behind it.

Who is eligible

  • Individuals only. One living person — never an organization, office, or team.
  • Ten years of verified work. The contribution must already be made. This award honours what has been done, never what is merely promised.
  • No age limit. A young person with a decade of work qualifies; there is no minimum age.
  • The unnoticed come first. Fame is not a bar, but between equals, the one the world has not yet thanked is preferred.
  • No serving officials. Those in political office are not eligible while serving, nor for three years after — so the award can never become a favour.
  • A youth path. Those aged roughly 15–29 with at least three years of work may be recognized on a separate Rising Gratitude track — a ladder toward a pillar, not the pillar itself.

The twelve fields

Every pillar is placed in a field, so the Directory can be searched by the kind of good a person did.

What counts as evidence

Every claim must be confirmed by at least two independent sources from at least two of these classes. A witness's word alone is never enough on its own — and the most important claims must be seen in person.

  • Documentary. Records made at the time by others — registers, payrolls, church and mosque and kebele records, dated media.
  • Independent testimony. People with nothing to gain, unconnected to the nominee and the witnesses — officials, elders, health workers, those served.
  • Close testimony. The witnesses who knew the work. They assert; independent evidence confirms.
  • On the ground. What a trained verifier can see, measure, count, and photograph at the place of the work.
  • Oral testimony. For those who do not write, recorded and read back — of equal standing. No one is shut out for lack of paper.
The same bar, fairly met. A rural life with little paperwork is not disadvantaged. The proof required is the same; only the forms it may take differ.

How the work is weighed

Adjudicators score only what has been verified, against published measures. Character proven through sustained, selfless service outweighs sheer scale — this protects the village teacher of two hundred children against the promoter of "two hundred thousand reached."

  • Depth of impact — 25%
  • Duration & consistency — 20%
  • Selflessness — 20%
  • Verified beneficiary outcomes — 15%
  • Reach & multiplication — 10%
  • Originality under constraint — 10%

The seven stages

Between a nomination and a pillar stand seven stages. AI helps at stages two through four; a named human signs every consequential decision, and stage six happens in a room with no AI at all.

  1. Nomination — by three close witnesses, in any Ethiopian language, written or spoken.
  2. Screening — AI checks completeness and independence; a named officer signs every decision, and helps incomplete nominations rather than rejecting them.
  3. Consent — the person is told, and nothing proceeds without their written agreement.
  4. Cross-verification — each claim is broken down and tested against independent evidence and public record.
  5. Field verification — a trained verifier from another region visits the work in person and speaks with those it touched; a second reviewer checks the first.
  6. Adjudication — an independent council decides in an AI-free room, by a two-thirds majority, and may award no one.
  7. Publication — the verified record and the reasons are published, and the pillar joins the Directory.

The balanced record

A pillar is not a monument of flattery. Every record tells the whole of a life in stages: what was built and verified, why it went unseen, what failed and what it cost — in the person's own words — the lessons the life offers humanity, and what they believed at the end of each stage, recorded verbatim and never edited. Honesty is a condition of the honour: to accept a pillar is to let the failures be told alongside the triumphs, so the young learn the truth of a good life, not a legend.
